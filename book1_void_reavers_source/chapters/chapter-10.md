# Chapter 10: The Corporate Armada

The boardroom of the Hyperion Transport Consortium occupied the entire top floor of their Earth headquarters, its transparent walls offering a view of the Pacific Ocean three hundred stories below. Around the obsidian conference table sat the most powerful corporate executives in human space, their combined wealth exceeding the GDP of most colony worlds.

"The situation is intolerable," declared CEO Margaret Hastings, her voice sharp enough to cut steel. "First, pirates expose our subsidiary's banking fraud. Then they save a quantum research facility from our own stupidity. And now they're claiming to have defeated some kind of alien threat? They're making us look like incompetents."

"With respect, Margaret," interjected Director Hamilton of Quantum Dynamics, "they did save my facility. And according to our deep space monitoring stations, something anomalous did occur in the asteroid belt three days ago."

"Per our threat assessment protocols," Bradford said — the security chief who'd once threatened Captain Elisabeth Chen — "the pirate activity in that sector doesn't correlate with any known anomalous events. Our risk matrix classifies the 'alien contact' claim as unverified hostile propaganda, confidence level ninety-two percent."

Diana Marsh sat quietly at the far end of the table, her corporate liaison cover intact despite her dealings with the pirates. She watched the executives argue, filing away every detail for her real employers—whoever they were this week.

"Enough," said a new voice, and the room fell silent. Adrianus Voss, Chairman of the Board and the richest human being in recorded history, rarely attended meetings in person. His presence here spoke volumes about the gravity of the situation. "Ms. Marsh, you've had dealings with these pirates. What's your assessment?"

Diana chose her words carefully. "Captain Vega and her allied crews represent a new evolution in piracy. They're organized, principled to a degree, and surprisingly effective. If they claim to have encountered something alien, I'd be inclined to investigate rather than dismiss it."

"Investigate?" Hastings laughed. "They're criminals. Terrorists. We should be destroying them, not investigating their delusions."

"Then perhaps it's time we did just that," Voss said quietly. "Mr. Bradford, what would it take to eliminate the pirate threat once and for all?"

Bradford had clearly been waiting for this question. He activated a holographic display — fleet distributions, logistics chains, operational timelines, all colour-coded by risk tier. "Per the mobilisation framework I submitted last quarter, with combined corporate security forces — Hyperion, Quantum Dynamics, Titan Industrial, TDI — we can assemble a fleet of two hundred vessels within six weeks. Commander Harrington at TDI has already indicated his corvette divisions are available for redeployment."

"The cost?" someone asked.

"Eight hundred billion credits, inclusive of operational expenses, hazard differentials, and post-engagement liability provisions." Bradford pulled up a cost-benefit slide. "Annual piracy losses exceed that figure. ROI positive within twenty-four months, per the model."

Hastings cut in. "Skip the spreadsheets, Bradford. Can you kill them or not?"

"The model indicates—"

"Yes or no."

"Yes."

"And the governments?" Diana asked. "Earth, Mars, the colony federations—they won't sit idle while we assemble a private military force that size."

"The governments are stretched thin dealing with colonial unrest and economic instability," Voss replied. "Besides, we'll frame it as a security operation to protect commerce. They'll thank us for handling a problem they can't afford to address."

The vote was almost unanimous. Within the hour, orders went out across human space. Corporate security forces began to mobilize, mercenary companies received lucrative contracts, and shipyards started rushing military refits. The Corporate Armada was born.

In the asteroid belt, Captain Zara Vega received word of the corporate mobilization from an unexpected source.

"Diana Marsh," she said, recognizing the encrypted signature. "I wondered when you'd surface again."

The message was brief but chilling: "Corporate alliance forming massive fleet. Objective: Total elimination of piracy. You have perhaps six weeks before they're ready to move. The alien threat you reported has been dismissed as fiction. They're coming for you all. - A friend."

Zara read the message twice, then called an emergency meeting of her senior staff.

"Two hundred ships?" Jax said after she'd briefed them. "That's not a fleet, that's an invasion force."

"It's worse than that," Chen added, running calculations. "If they coordinate properly, they could blockade every pirate haven simultaneously. Cut off our supplies, starve us out, then pick us off at their leisure."

"So we don't let them coordinate," Kira suggested. "Hit them before they're ready, while they're still assembling."

"With what?" Malik asked. "Even with the mutual defence pact, we maybe have sixty ships total. And half of those are converted freighters, not warships."

Zara studied the star charts spread across the table. Sixty ships against two hundred. It was impossible odds, the kind that got people killed. But running wasn't an option either. The corporations wouldn't stop until every pirate was dead or captured.

"We need allies," she decided. "The independent colonies, the free traders, anyone who doesn't want to see the corporations controlling all space travel."

"They won't fight for pirates," Jax pointed out.

"No, but they might fight against corporate monopoly." Zara highlighted several systems on the map. "These colonies have been resisting corporate takeover for years. If we can convince them that the Armada won't stop with pirates..."

"It's a long shot," Chen said.

"It's the only shot we have."

The next three weeks saw frantic activity across the outer systems. Pirate envoys visited independent colonies, carrying evidence of corporate overreach and promises of mutual support. Some doors slammed shut immediately. Others opened cautiously, colony leaders willing to at least hear what the pirates had to say.

The breakthrough came at Proxima Station, a fiercely independent trading hub that had resisted corporate influence for decades. Governor Sarah Kozel, a former freight captain who'd built the station from nothing, listened to Zara's proposal with growing interest.

"You're asking me to ally with pirates against the most powerful economic forces in human space," she summarized. "That's either very brave or very stupid."

"Maybe both," Zara admitted. "But consider this—once the corporations have their Armada, what's to stop them from using it against anyone who defies them? Today it's pirates. Tomorrow it could be independent stations that refuse to pay their tariffs."

Kozel was silent for a long moment. Then: "I've seen what happens when the corporations get too much power. Worlds stripped bare, populations enslaved by debt, freedom sacrificed for profit. If you're right about this Armada..."

"I am. Here's the intelligence we've gathered." Zara transmitted the data Diana had provided, plus what pirate networks had uncovered. Ship movements, supply requisitions, mercenary contracts—all pointing to a massive military buildup.

"Damn," Kozel breathed. "They're really doing it." She looked up at Zara. "You know who else should see this? Captain Elisabeth Chen — Hyperion freighter captain, runs the *Stellar Mule* on the outer routes. She's been feeding the independent stations intel on corporate shipping patterns for months. Quietly. Using her own encryption."

"I know Chen," Zara said. She didn't elaborate.

"She's docked here now. Said something about a psych eval she decided not to show up for." Kozel sent for her.

Elisabeth Chen walked into the conference room twenty minutes later. She stopped when she saw Zara, recognition crossing her face — not the pirate who'd robbed her, but the one who'd fixed her ship and left a two-word message.

"Captain Vega."

"Captain Chen."

Neither of them said anything else about it. Elisabeth sat down, read the intelligence data, and said: "Hyperion's Commander Harrington from TDI is leading the corvette screening force. I served on a Hyperion ship with his second officer. She'll follow orders right up until the moment she thinks they're illegal."

Kozel consulted the other independent governors. Two said no — New Shanghai and Barnard's Colony, both too dependent on corporate trade contracts to risk it. The rest said yes, some grudgingly, some with the relief of people who'd been waiting for someone to go first.

The Free Colony Alliance took shape over four days — not two, because real diplomacy was slower than anyone wanted. It wasn't enough to match the corporate forces, but it was a start.

More importantly, it changed the conversation. Media outlets across human space picked up the story. Suddenly, the corporations weren't just hunting pirates—they were threatening the independence of free colonies. Public opinion, already soured by decades of corporate scandals, began to shift.

In the Hyperion boardroom, Adrianus Voss watched the media coverage with cold fury.

"This was supposed to be a simple police action," he said, his voice dangerously quiet. "Now we're being painted as tyrants."

"It's Vega," Bradford said. "Per our network analysis, she's the single-point-of-failure in the alliance command structure. Neutralise her and the coalition fragments within seventy-two hours."

"Then neutralise her," Voss said. "Whatever it takes."

"I'll activate the standing contract. The Port Liberty operation will require—"

"I said whatever it takes, Bradford. Don't bring me the process. Bring me the result."

The assassination attempt came five days later.

Zara was walking from the conference room to the docking bay on Port Liberty, Jax two steps behind. The corridor was empty — maintenance shift, most of the station at dinner. She noticed the wrong thing first: the ventilation grille on the ceiling had been removed and replaced with the screws facing the wrong direction.

"Down," she said, and dropped.

The first shot hit the wall where her head had been. Jax was already firing, his shotgun booming in the narrow corridor. Two figures in maintenance coveralls came around the corner ahead, weapons up. Corporate-issue sidearms, the kind Hyperion Security carried.

Jax killed the first one. Zara rolled behind a support strut and put three shots into the second. A third attacker came from behind the ventilation panel above — she'd been right about the screws — and Jax caught him with his off hand as the man dropped.

Twelve seconds, start to finish. Three dead attackers. Station security cameras had caught everything.

"Bradford's work," Jax said, checking the bodies. Hyperion employee IDs, badly scrubbed but readable under the right scanner.

Governor Kozel broadcast the footage system-wide within the hour. "They tried to murder a negotiator during peace talks. Is this the future the corporations offer?"

The Corporate Armada launched two weeks ahead of schedule, their plans disrupted but not derailed. One hundred and eighty-seven ships, the largest fleet assembled since the Colonial Wars, began moving toward the asteroid belt.

Against them stood the Pirate-Colonial Alliance: sixty-three pirate vessels and forty-two colonial defence ships, supplemented by converted freighters and mining vessels. One hundred and five ships total, most inferior to their corporate counterparts.

The two fleets met at Vesta, the second-largest asteroid in the belt. Zara had chosen the battlefield carefully—the asteroid's gravity well and debris field would neutralize some of the Armada's numerical advantage.

"All ships, this is Admiral Chen Wei of the Free Colony Alliance," her former pilot announced. He'd been elected to command the combined fleet, a pirate leading colonists into battle. "Today we fight not for plunder or profit, but for freedom itself. The corporations would make slaves of us all, pirates and colonists alike. We stand here to say: No more."

The battle began at extreme range, corporate dreadnoughts launching waves of missiles while alliance ships used the asteroids for cover. The superior corporate firepower told immediately, several lighter pirate vessels vanishing in nuclear fire.

But Zara had one more card to play. Chen had spent three weeks in drydock after the mutiny reverse-engineering the Prometheus data — the targeted broadcast that had paralysed the alien scout had worked, but fried the comms array doing it. He'd figured out how to generate a smaller, portable version: not a weapon, but a disruption field that scrambled targeting systems. Rodriguez's engineers had replicated the design across the alliance fleet during the buildup.

"Execute Prometheus Protocol," she commanded.

Across the alliance fleet, modified field generators came online. It couldn't fold space like a true quantum drive, but it could wreak havoc on enemy targeting systems.

Suddenly, corporate weapons that never missed were flying wide. Sensor locks failed and reformed randomly. The organized Armada assault devolved into chaos as their technological advantage evaporated.

"All ships, attack pattern omega!" Zara commanded. "Hit them while they're blind!"

The alliance fleet surged forward, pouring fire into the confused corporate lines. Pirate raiders, used to fighting in chaotic melees, adapted quickly. Colonial defence ships, crewed by people defending their homes, fought with desperate fury.

The *Crimson Revenge* led a squadron directly at the corporate flagship, a massive dreadnought that dwarfed any three pirate vessels. But size meant nothing when your weapons couldn't lock on target.

"Rodriguez, you seeing this?" Zara called to her wingman.

"I see it, Bloodhawk!" Maria Rodriguez, recovered from her injuries and commanding a new ship, laughed over the comm. "Let's show these corporate bastards how pirates dance!"

The battle raged for six hours. Ships died on both sides, fire and vacuum claiming the brave and cowardly alike. But slowly, impossibly, the alliance began to gain ground. The corporate fleet, trained for orderly engagements, couldn't adapt to the quantum-disrupted chaos.

Then, just as victory seemed possible, space itself tore open.

The alien ship that emerged dwarfed both fleets combined. It was a kilometer-long needle of impossible angles and flowing metal, and it announced its presence by vaporising three corporate dreadnoughts with a single weapon discharge.

"All ships, all ships!" Zara broadcast on every frequency. "Cease fire! We have a bigger problem!"

The battle stopped as suddenly as it had begun, both fleets turning to face this new threat. The alien vessel hung motionless for a moment, scanning, evaluating. Then it broadcast a message in perfect, if oddly accented, human speech:

"Primitive species designated 'Human.' You have destroyed a survey unit. You have accessed forbidden technologies. You will cease expansion immediately or face sterilization. Compliance time: one solar rotation. This is your only warning."

The message repeated three times, then the alien ship simply vanished, leaving a quantum wake that set every sensor screaming.

In the silence that followed, corporate and alliance ships drifted among the debris of their battle, united by a threat that made their conflict seem suddenly, terrifyingly petty.

"Admiral Voss," Zara transmitted to the corporate flagship. "I think we need to talk."

The response came after a long pause. Not from Voss, but from Bradford. His voice had lost the boardroom polish. He sounded like a man standing in a room where the air smelled like burned circuitry and dead people.

"Admiral Voss is dead. The alien weapon — he was on the forward observation deck. I saw it happen." A breath. "I'm in command now. And yes, Captain Vega, I think we need to talk."

Zara looked at the tactical display. Corporate ships and alliance ships, drifting together among the debris of their battle, all of them facing the empty space where the alien had been.

"Then talk," she said.
