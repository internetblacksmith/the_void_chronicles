# Chapter 4: The Space Trucker's Lament

Captain Elisabeth Chen sat in the ruins of her cargo hold, surrounded by the scattered remnants of what had been a forty-million-credit shipment. The pirates had been efficient but not cruel—they'd taken what they wanted and left just enough for the *Stellar Mule* to limp to the nearest station. It was more than most crews could expect from an encounter with raiders, but that didn't make the loss any easier to bear.

"Status report," she called to Yuki Tanaka, her engineer, who was running the damage assessment from the far end of the hold.

"Could be worse, Captain." Yuki didn't look up from her datapad. "Primary systems intact. We lost seventy percent of the cargo, but they left us fuel and supplies to reach Neptune Station. No casualties."

Elisabeth nodded, running her fingers through her greying hair. At forty-five, she'd been running cargo for over two decades, and this was her third encounter with pirates. The first two had been... different. More final.

"What about communications?"

"They fried our long-range transmitter, but short-range is still functional. We won't be able to report in until we're within a few million clicks of the station."

Smart. The pirates—Captain Vega's crew—had disabled their ability to call for help without crippling the ship entirely. It was almost considerate, in a twisted way.

"Captain." Yuki set down her datapad. "You need to see something in the engine room."

Elisabeth followed her to the engine room, where Yuki pointed to one of the secondary power couplings. A small device had been attached to it, blinking with a steady green light.

"Tracking device?" Elisabeth asked.

"No, ma'am. It's... it's a repair module. Military grade. Our coupling was showing stress fractures—I was planning to mention it at the next maintenance stop. This thing is holding it together, probably better than new."

Elisabeth stared at the device. "They fixed our ship?"

"Looks like it. There are three more on other systems that were marginal. Whoever did this knew exactly what they were looking at."

"And took time during a raid to fix them?" Elisabeth shook her head. "What kind of pirate does that?"

"The kind that spaced Kellerman for being a murderer," Yuki said. She was already running a diagnostic on the nearest module. "Captain — there's a parts manifest rerouted to our credit line. She charged the replacement couplings to *us*. To our own repair budget, so the billing system won't flag it as external."

Elisabeth stared. "She didn't just fix our ship. She hid the fix so Hyperion wouldn't notice."

They'd all seen that part—the brief, vicious battle between the two pirate ships. Elisabeth had been certain they were about to witness their own deaths when the second vessel appeared. Pirates fighting pirates over prey usually meant no witnesses left behind.

But Vega had surprised them. Instead of the massacre Elisabeth expected, they'd gotten a professional robbery and a warning about cooperating. It was almost civilized, if you ignored the theft of most of their cargo.

"Set course for Neptune Station," Elisabeth ordered. "Best speed we can manage. The sooner we report this, the sooner we can forget it happened."

But as the *Stellar Mule* limped through space, Elisabeth found herself unable to stop thinking about it. In the mess hall that evening, she brought it up with Yuki over station-brewed coffee that tasted like it had been filtered through a boot.

"I asked around on the repair crews' channel," Yuki said. "The *Kestrel's Hope* got hit by Vega six months ago. Their engineer told me she left them with a working atmo scrubber and a note — actual handwritten note — saying which parts to replace at their next stop."

"A note."

"Clipped to the intake manifold. Apparently she circled the corroded fitting in red marker."

Elisabeth shook her head. A pirate with a five-million-credit Hyperion bounty on her head, crawling through engine compartments during raids to fix things.

---

Two days out from Neptune Station, Yuki picked up a distress signal — an escape pod, bearing two-seven-mark-four, beacon identifying it as *Void Dancer*. Kellerman's ship.

Elisabeth stood on the bridge for a long minute, looking at the blip on the display. Protocol was clear: distress signals required response, regardless of the sender. Protocol had been written by people who'd never had to decide whether to rescue someone who might have killed them under different circumstances.

"Alter course," she said. "We pick them up."

They tractored in the pod six hours later. The occupant was a young man barely out of his teens — dehydrated, half-conscious, pirate tattoos on his forearms. He identified himself as Rico. He'd been in the *Void Dancer*'s maintenance bay when the torpedoes hit.

Once the medic stabilised him, Elisabeth sat with him in the small sickbay. He wouldn't look at her.

"I knew what Kellerman was," he said. "Jobs are hard to find in the outer systems. Piracy pays better than mining. I told myself it was just theft, that the violence was someone else's department." He picked at the edge of his blanket. "Three months ago we hit a passenger shuttle. Kellerman spaced the crew because they'd seen our faces. I was in the maintenance bay for that one too. Didn't see it happen. Just heard the airlocks cycle, one after another."

"And you stayed."

"Where was I going to go?" He looked at her for the first time. He had the eyes of someone much older than nineteen. "What happens to me now?"

Elisabeth didn't have a good answer.

Neptune Station was a sprawling commercial hub between the inner and outer systems. The *Stellar Mule* docked at a repair berth, her damage drawing looks from other crews.

Elisabeth made her report to the Hyperion Transport Consortium. The corporate security chief, Bradford, listened from behind a desk that had never seen vacuum.

"So Captain Vega protected you from another pirate," he said when she'd finished.

"She protected herself. We happened to benefit."

"And then robbed you."

"Yes."

"After repairing your ship."

"Yes."

Bradford leaned back. "This is the problem with transport captains. You start sympathising with criminals. Vega has cost this company millions. The fact that she's less of a monster than some others doesn't make her a hero."

"I'm not calling her a hero. I'm reporting what happened."

"And the pirate survivor you picked up?"

"Maritime law requires—"

"I know what the law requires." Bradford pulled up a file on his console — a file with Elisabeth's name on the header tab. "I also know we have a standing assassination contract on Vega, in addition to the bounty. If Vega thinks she's safe because transport captains are grateful to her, that's useful to us. We'd hate to lose that advantage because one of our own captains started singing her praises publicly."

The room went cold. Elisabeth had known Hyperion played rough. An assassination contract was something else.

"I'm not singing anyone's praises, Mr. Bradford."

"See that you don't. And Captain — the psych evaluation team will be in touch. Standard procedure for crews who've experienced traumatic piracy incidents." He smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "The company takes your wellbeing very seriously."

After the meeting, Elisabeth found a bar on the station's lower ring. The whiskey tasted like engine coolant. Yuki found her there an hour later.

"Bradford?"

"Assassination contract," Elisabeth said. "On Vega. Hyperion has a standing assassination contract on a pirate captain, and he told me about it to make sure I'd keep my mouth shut." She drank. "And a psych eval. For being too sympathetic to the woman who saved my crew."

Yuki ordered her own drink and didn't say anything for a while.

"I know a captain heading to the frontier," Yuki said finally. "She's always short on maintenance crew. No questions about backgrounds. Rico could start fresh."

"Do it. Use my credits."

Yuki nodded. "And what about you?"

"What about me?"

"You're thinking about quitting."

Elisabeth looked at her engineer. "Is it that obvious?"

"You've been hauling cargo for Hyperion for twenty-two years. They've never once come out here to see what it's actually like. And now they're telling you they have assassination contracts on people who fix your ship." Yuki shrugged. "I'd be thinking about it too."

"I'm not quitting. Not yet. But I'm not forgetting what Bradford said, either."

The next morning, Rico left on the frontier ship. He tried to thank Elisabeth. She told him to stay out of trouble and walked away before it turned into something she'd have to feel.

An hour before departure, an encrypted message arrived on her personal console. No sender ID, routed through a dozen relays.

Two words: "You're welcome."

Elisabeth deleted the message. Then she sat in her captain's chair and watched the undocking sequence, the docking clamps releasing one by one, the *Stellar Mule* drifting free.

She had a ship. She had a crew. She had a company that put assassination contracts on people. Something would have to give, eventually. But not today.

Today she had cargo to haul.
