# Void Reavers — Voice Bible

Working rules for revision. This file is the prose-level standard every edited chapter must conform to. When in doubt, consult this before writing.

## Purpose

The current manuscript has no voice. Every sentence could come from any chapter. The fix is not "make it fancier" — it's to **eliminate specific tics** that flatten the prose and **enforce concrete specificity** in place of abstract thematic gesture.

This file defines:
1. Banned patterns (with evidence from the current text)
2. Required cadence rules
3. The chapter-ending rule (non-negotiable)
4. Dialogue rules
5. Per-character voice registers
6. Sample good/bad sentences from the manuscript
7. A revision checklist

---

## 1. Banned patterns

Every item below appears in the current manuscript. None of it survives revision.

### 1.1 Banned similes and phrases

- **"Predatory smile"** — and all "her smile turned X" descriptors. Women do not smile predatorily in this book anymore. (See ch1:7, ch6:25, ch3:41.) If a character is about to be cruel, show the cruelty, not the smile.
- **"Like a bat out of hell"** (ch6:141) — dead metaphor.
- **"Like wolves on wounded prey"** (ch2:241) — dead metaphor.
- **"Like a metallic tumor" / "bulged like tumors"** (ch2:3, ch1:105) — the manuscript uses "tumor" twice as a simile within two chapters. No more tumors. No more biomedical similes for ships or stations unless a character would actually think that way.
- **"Riding low in the void"** / **"all bulk and no beauty"** — generic space-opera filler. Cut.

### 1.2 Banned sentence constructions

- **"In the [abstract noun] between [things], only [adjective] [nouns] [verb]."**
  - Current: *"In the lawless void between the stars, only the strong survived."* (ch1:211)
  - Current: *"In the void between stars, something was always better than nothing."* (ch4:173)
  - Current: *"In the void between stars, legends were born from such moments."* (ch1:216)
  - **Rule:** Zero aphorisms about "the void." Zero.

- **"And [Character] intended to [verb] [adverb]."**
  - Current: *"And Zara intended to survive for a very long time."* (ch1:213)
  - Current: *"The game was changing, and Zara intended to help write the new rules."* (ch5:267)
  - **Rule:** Characters don't announce their intentions in narration. Show the intent through action.

- **"Not just X but Y"** and **"X, Y, and Z" triadic thematic escalation**
  - Current: *"It was the wisdom to impose them on yourself, the strength to choose sacrifice over selfishness, the courage to grow without consuming."* (ch19:121)
  - Current: *"Pirates and princes, thieves and heroes."* (ch12:205)
  - **Rule:** Triadic constructions allowed only when the three items are concrete, different, and non-redundant. No triads of abstract nouns.

- **"The age of X had begun" / "The age of X was over"**
  - Current: *"The age of reckless expansion was over. The age of probation had begun."* (ch11:151)
  - **Rule:** No "age of" sentences. History books talk like that; characters don't.

- **"The adventure continued" / "The game had changed"**
  - Current: *"The game had changed. Time to learn the new rules."* (ch11:157)
  - **Rule:** No cliffhanger coda lines that tell the reader what's next. Let scenes end on silence or specifics.

### 1.3 Banned abstract nouns (at sentence-ending positions)

These words are permitted in the narrative but **cannot close a sentence, paragraph, or chapter**:

- legacy, reputation, legend, destiny, wisdom
- potential, transformation, journey
- "the stars" (when used as poetic flourish, not literal)
- "the void" (same)
- "the universe" (when used as emotional stand-in)
- "everything changed" / "nothing would ever be the same"

**Test:** If a sentence ends on one of these words, it's probably thematic self-summary. Rewrite to end on a concrete image or a verb.

### 1.4 Banned summary phrases

- **"Months passed."** (ch16:93)
- **"Five years had passed."** (ch12:3)
- **"Three months into her new life, Zara..."** (ch3:191)
- **"As the months passed, Zara threw herself into learning..."** (ch3:211)

**Rule:** A time skip of more than a few days requires either (a) a dramatized compression scene showing the change happening or (b) a hard chapter break where the next chapter opens in the new time without announcing it. The phrase "X passed" is a confession that the author skipped the hard part.

### 1.5 Banned adverb-as-tone-bearer

- **"Said bitterly / simply / grimly / coldly / darkly / softly"** — used as shortcut for emotion the dialogue should already contain.
- **Rule:** Default to "said." Use other tags only when the action is genuinely non-obvious. If a line needs "bitterly" to land, the line is wrong.

### 1.6 Banned word: "quantum" as magic suffix

"Quantum" is allowed when it refers to a specific technology with established rules. It is banned when it functions as "the plot needs this to work":

- "quantum drive" — OK, it's a named tech
- "quantum field manipulation" — OK if the rules are consistent
- "quantum signature spiked" — lazy, reads as "the magic number went up"
- "broadcast quantum equations at it" — this is the current climax of ch9 and it's embarrassing
- "quantum counter-resonance" — hand-wave

**Rule:** Every use of "quantum" must pass the test: *Could this be replaced with "magic" without changing the scene?* If yes, rewrite until no.

## 2. Cadence rules

### 2.1 Sentence rhythm

- **Vary sentence length.** The current manuscript runs in clumps of 15-22 word sentences with near-identical rhythm. Mix short (4-8 words) with long (25+).
- **End paragraphs on short sentences.** The beat should land.
- **Avoid the climbing tricolon.** The pattern "X, Y, and finally Z-that-is-biggest" is overused to the point of mechanism.

### 2.2 Paragraph shape

- **Don't cap every paragraph with a summary line.** The current book does this constantly — the paragraph describes an event, then a summary sentence tells you what the event meant. Cut the summary sentence. Trust the reader.
- **Let paragraphs end on objects and actions.** "He set the cup down." Not: "The moment hung between them, pregnant with meaning."

### 2.3 Point-of-view discipline

- **Third-limited, grounded in Zara** for most chapters. Other POVs only when the chapter demands it (ch4 currently uses Elisabeth Chen's POV — this is one of the book's best moments, *preserve this*).
- **No head-hopping inside a scene.** The current book slips between characters' interiors mid-paragraph. Pick one head per scene.

## 3. The chapter-ending rule (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

**Every chapter must end on a concrete image or a specific character action. No abstractions, no thematic pronouncements, no "the stars looked different now."**

Current ending patterns that will be deleted:

- *"The age of probation had begun."* (ch11)
- *"The adventure continued."* (ch20)
- *"The game had changed. Time to learn the new rules."* (ch11:157)
- *"In the lawless void between the stars, only the strong survived. And Zara intended to survive for a very long time."* (ch1:211-213)

What a chapter ending should look like (invented examples for the register):

- *She closed the comm channel and sat in the dark, counting her breaths until her hands stopped shaking.*
- *Jax passed her the bottle without looking at her. She drank.*
- *The last of the fuel gauge blinked amber. Zara reached for the course plot.*

**Test:** If the final sentence could be printed on a movie poster, it's wrong. Rewrite until it couldn't.

## 4. Dialogue rules

### 4.1 No speechifying

The current book has characters make thesis-statement speeches. Zara's ch11:97 monologue (*"I'm a pirate... I steal, I fight, I survive. But I also protect those who can't protect themselves..."*) is the book's most embarrassing moment because she says exactly what the author wants the reader to think. **Characters do not state the theme.**

### 4.2 No info-dump-as-conversation

Current: ch9:29-35, Krieger briefing the Pirate Council. Every line is exposition dressed as dialogue. If information needs to reach the reader, either (a) dramatize the discovery of the information or (b) put it in narration, not in a character's mouth.

### 4.3 Characters must disagree

In the current manuscript, characters agree with Zara too easily. Every scene has "X said. Y added. Z agreed." Real conversations have friction. At least one scene per chapter should contain a disagreement that isn't resolved by a single clever line.

### 4.4 No villain monologues from the 1990s action-movie template

Current ch12:37 (Jonas Steele): *"For five years, we've watched humanity grovel before alien masters. We've seen proud pirates become lapdogs."*

Nobody talks like this. Antagonists need specific grievances, specific idioms, specific ways of being wrong. Steele needs a rewrite from scratch.

## 5. Per-character voice registers

Each main character needs a distinct voice. If you cover the attributions in a multi-character scene, the reader should be able to guess who's speaking. Current draft fails this test completely.

### Zara Vega
- **Register:** Pragmatic, terse, occasional dark humor. Doesn't philosophize. Doesn't explain herself.
- **Tics:** Short declarative sentences. Dismissive of compliments. Answers questions with questions when cornered.
- **Banned:** No introspective monologues. No "she thought about her past." Her past shows through what she refuses to talk about, not through what she announces.

### Jax (first mate)
- **Register:** Older than Zara, more superstitious, uses pirate slang Zara avoids. Cybernetic eye — reference it through action (adjusts it, it hums, it glitches in stress), never through narration.
- **Tics:** Speaks in negatives ("that's not how this goes"). Interrupts. Uses Zara's name more than she uses his.
- **Banned:** No "grizzled veteran" mode. No life lessons.

### Chen Wei (engineer → quantum specialist)
- **Register:** Precise, technical, slightly uncomfortable with violence. Uses more words than anyone else. When he talks, it's because he's figured something out and needs to say it fast before he forgets.
- **Tics:** Starts sentences with "actually" or "the thing is." Corrects himself mid-sentence. Gets quieter when scared, not louder.
- **Banned:** No suddenly-becoming-a-warrior moments. Chen is the one who doesn't fight well, and that's a feature.

### Kira (young crewmember)
- **Register:** The youngest voice on the ship. Wants to be older than she is. Watches Zara and copies her, badly.
- **Tics:** Overcompensates with toughness, then breaks when she can't hold it. Calls senior crew by their last names when she's nervous.
- **Banned:** No "wide-eyed innocent." No "surprisingly capable for her age" narration.

### Maria Rodriguez
- **Register:** Zara's mirror — what Zara could become if she'd stayed ethical from the start. Thinks in systems where Zara thinks tactically. Businesswoman pirate — sees sustainability, long-term thinking.
- **Tics:** Uses "we" where Zara uses "I." Counts costs in ways Zara doesn't bother with.
- **Preserve:** Her "sustainable harvest, why kill the golden goose" line (ch5:67) is the one moment she becomes real. Expand from there.

### Bradford (corporate antagonist → reluctant ally)
- **Register:** Corporate-bred, precise diction, tries to sound more in control than he is. His arc from enemy to ally needs an earned scene — currently happens off-screen.
- **Tics:** Uses business language even in crisis ("acceptable losses," "cost-benefit"). Hates being surprised.
- **Banned:** No "I see now that pirates are the real heroes" redemption speech. His turn should be reluctant and incomplete.

### The Watchers (aliens)
- **Register:** Currently reads as legal-document bullet points in the harmonic-voice gimmick. Keep the uncanny cadence but make it specific. They need idioms that don't translate cleanly. They need to misunderstand human things in ways that reveal their own framework.
- **Banned:** No "Species: Human. Technology level: Primitive." This is *Star Trek TOS* alien-logic parody. Give them a grammar that feels genuinely not-human.

## 6. Sample sentences from the manuscript

### 6.1 Good — preserve this register

These are the moments where the current draft works. Study them. When in doubt, write more like these.

- **ch3:163** — *"She did the only thing she could think of. She bit him."*
  - **Why it works:** Physical, specific, short, funny, surprising. Zero adjectives. Zero abstractions.

- **ch4:27** — *"They fixed our ship?"*
  - **Why it works:** Theme delivered through a question a character actually asks, not a thematic declaration. Elisabeth Chen realizes what Vega did without the narrator telling us.

- **ch5:67** — *"Sustainable harvest. Why kill the golden goose?"* (Rodriguez)
  - **Why it works:** Reveals character through a single unexpected framing. Rodriguez becomes a person in one line.

- **ch6 Hoffman confession broadcast** (scene as a whole)
  - **Why it works:** A reversal that dramatizes the theme (corruption exposed by its own tools) instead of announcing it.

### 6.2 Bad — eliminate this register

- **ch1:211-213** — *"In the lawless void between the stars, only the strong survived. And Zara intended to survive for a very long time."*
  - **Diagnosis:** Aphorism + character-intention coda. Exactly the pattern every chapter currently ends on.

- **ch11:151** — *"The stars looked different now, Zara realised. No longer empty spaces to be conquered, but inhabited by watchers who'd judge humanity's every step. The age of reckless expansion was over. The age of probation had begun."*
  - **Diagnosis:** Every sentence is a thematic declaration. No image, no action. Four consecutive abstractions. Delete the entire passage and replace with something Zara does physically.

- **ch14:125** — *"I killed, I destroyed, I did whatever Morrison demanded because the alternative was death."*
  - **Diagnosis:** Character reciting her own Wikipedia summary. Trauma doesn't work this way. (This chapter is being cut in the new shape, but the pattern repeats elsewhere.)

- **ch20:115** — *"The adventure continued."*
  - **Diagnosis:** Book refusing to end. (This chapter is being cut, but: no sentence like this survives in the new book.)

## 7. Revision checklist

Run this on every edited chapter before considering it done:

- [ ] No chapter-ending aphorism. Ends on concrete image or action.
- [ ] No "predatory smile," no dead similes, no tumor-ships.
- [ ] No "in the void between X, only Y" constructions.
- [ ] No "[Character] intended to [verb]" narration.
- [ ] No "X passed" time-skip phrases.
- [ ] No "said bitterly/grimly/simply" where dialogue should carry the tone.
- [ ] No abstract-noun sentence endings (legacy, destiny, wisdom, etc.).
- [ ] No theme-statement speeches from characters.
- [ ] No info-dump dialogue.
- [ ] At least one moment of actual disagreement in the chapter.
- [ ] Voice test: cover the dialogue tags. Can you tell who is speaking from register alone?
- [ ] "Quantum" test: every use of "quantum" could not be replaced with "magic."
- [ ] Sentence-length variation: at least one sentence under 8 words, at least one over 25, in every scene.
- [ ] Zara does not philosophize. She acts.
- [ ] The chapter contains at least one specific sensory detail (smell, sound, texture — not just sight).

---

## 8. When in doubt

- **Concrete beats abstract.** Always.
- **Silence beats summary.** Always.
- **Specific beats general.** Always.
- **Showing a character's contradiction beats explaining it.** Always.
- **If it sounds like a movie poster, it's wrong.**
